Most Australians think their home is worth more in 2016, survey finds

Jan 25, 2016 Kirsten RobbData Journalist
Most Australian homeowners are under the impression their house got more expensive last year.

Most Australians think their home is worth more off the back of last years property boom, despite price gainsin 2015 largely being concentrated to the Sydney and Melbourne markets and clear signs that growth hadcooledbythe end of 2015.

A survey of2000 homeownersby law firm Slater and Gordonfound 78 per cent of Australiansbelieved the value of their propertyhad increased over the past 12 months.

Of those, almost a third said they would consider selling up to cash in on theprice growth.

Sydney and Melbourne were the two standout markets of 2015, while other capital cities largely remained flat or fell backwards. Photo:Josh Robenstone

Almost every respondent from Sydney (94 per cent) believed the price of theirproperty had been given a boost, while 89 per cent of Melburnians thought the same.

The response comes off the back of strong year of price growthin both cities, which ultimately cooled in the last months of theyear.

Auction clearance rates slipped to 61 per centand 69 per centin Sydney and Melbourne respectively by the last week of the auction calender,according to Domain Group data.

One-third of homeowners say they would consider selling to cash in on last years price growth.

Despite hitting a million dollar median earlier in the year, by theSeptemberquarterSydneys house price growth slowed tojust 3.2 per cent the lowest quarterly rate of growth since March 2014.

Melbourne house prices had gradually slowedby theSeptember quarter,but werepredicted to moderate even further by the end of December.

House price growth in Brisbane was moderate, Adelaideremainedflat and Perth prices had continued to fall by the September quarter.

The amount of construction in a suburb can affect supply and demand dynamics, having a major impact on price growth. Photo:Erin Jonasson

Despite this,76 per cent of Brisbane homeowners, 78 per cent of Adelaide owners and 57 per cent of Perth owners believedtheyd made gainson their property.

Ray White chief executive Brian White said Sydney and Melbourneclearly outperformedother capital citieslast year, but believedlow interest rates created a national impression that the underlying forces of the property market were strong.

Often the perception of interest ratemovements isthe most importantfactor for people, Mr White said.

He said inner-city markets in most capital cities had made a comeback last year and had forged ahead in most markets.

Mathew Tiller, national research manager at LJ Hooker, said markets should be looked at from a local level.

Factorssuch as the strength of thelocal economy,jobs andconstruction, affecthowthe property market performs, Mr Tiller said.

Over the past year, he said, the capital cities of miningand resource states hadunderperformed, whilemarketsin Sydney and Melbourne hadrelied on thefinance and constructionindustry fora boost.

What people need to realise is thesupply and demand dynamic how much construction is going on andwhat the future supply pipeline is in their suburb,and ifdemand in those areas willsupport construction, Mr Tiller said.

If theres not alot ofconstructionand a lot of demand, thatsa positive signfor future price growth.
